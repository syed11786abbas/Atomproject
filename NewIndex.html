"We humans are worst estimators of risk- either we underestimate or overestimate it. Who understands risk and act appropriately is called Rationale and its opposite is called Irrational. I have read this line so many time in variety of books, expressed in various ways and context. Till now (after living 25 years of life) I can confidently state that I am a irrational person in various aspects of my life. Indeed, when it comes to investing, I am over-estimator of risk and Charlie Munger quote would describe me best- "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there". During the lock-down and WFH period started, I observed a lot of valuation model like DCF and LBO models were uploaded on LinkedIn by various analysts of good companies (good companies means when you say the name in a social gathering, you will draw good respect and admiration, what you work there does not matter). Reason for uploading may be to draw more respect and admiration, I don't know and don't want to know. This really surprised me as valuation models are always kept private like a dark and dirty secret of an individual. Not to be exposed."
What surprised me more was the casualness they adopted to measure the risk aspect to be specific-poor scenario and sensitivity analysis, factoring of margin of error was missing and most importantly none of the model had a hint of randomness (a simple use RANDBETWEEN function in excel on your key variables can do magic). DCF models are in vogue. I love DCF valuation model, it gives me sense to meditate on company's intrinsic value. Albeit I am aware of the fact that DCF model is highly flawed, as terminal value is everything if you are valuing company on perpetuity basis (I am not comfortable of using multiples-EV/EBITDA, EV/blah-blah in my model as these multiples can be a reflection of irrationality). For example- If we assume a perpetual growth rate of 5% and a cost of capital of 9% then the terminal multiple is 25x. However, if we are off by one percent on either or both of our inputs, then the terminal multiple can range from 16x to 50x! (Source). You know what I am talking about, how we change and play with few variables on excels to meet our expected intrinsic value (Dirty Secret).

"“Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. He who understands it, earns it. he who doesn't ... pays it.” ― Albert Einstein Simple thing can create wonders which cannot be captured by a rational human mind. Everybody understood this in 5th grade mathematics class and even we cleared our unit test but failed to apply in our life. The concept of compounding could be explained through simple example- Samarth had Rs 100 he took this money and invested in fixed deposit which gave him interest rate of 10% on compounded basis. After a year he will get interest of Rs 10, so he will be having Rs 110 after a year. If he decides to keep the policy for next 10 years, at the end of the day he would have Rs 259.3 and interest earned will be Rs 159.3. This is the concept of compound interest, which in simple terms means interest on interest. The concept of compounding can also be applied on our twitter following also. Consider this example: If you have 1,000 followers on a Twitter and 10 of those people share your status update and if each of those ten has 1,000 followers, your follower numbers can reach up to 100,000. "
There are more than thousands of examples which show the power of compound interest in Indian financial market. One small example of compounding could be illustrated using our Nifty50. In the given table we have illustrated how Rs 1000 invested in 1990 could have turn into Rs 19,143 by the end of 2017, a total return of close to 1814% and CAGR of 12% in last 28 years. This return is after dot.com bubble, world biggest financial crisis, Satyam case, elections, many DHFL’s alike cases, Modiji’s speech’s and many more. I know many of you would be thinking how could we have invested in Nifty50 in 1990 when there were no index funds, it is simple you could have allocated in all Nifty50 stocks at that time in 1990. Even if you would have invested in 1995, your Rs 1,000 could have turned into Rs 7,155, a total return of 616% and CAGR of 9% in 23 years. And even after that if you would have invested in the century year 2000, your Rs 1,000 could have turned into Rs 6,039, a total return of 504% and CAGR of 11% in 18 years.
